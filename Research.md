# Signovate — Concept & Research

## The Problem

India has the largest deaf population globally — an estimated 18 to 20 million people. Out of this, fewer than 1% have access to education or training in Indian Sign Language (ISL). The number of certified ISL interpreters is extremely scarce, leaving millions of deaf individuals isolated not just at home, but in hospitals, banks, schools, and workplaces.

---

## Who we are building for

Most sign language apps target deaf users. Signovate targets the hearing people around them — the ones who, if they knew even 20-30 signs, could transform the daily experience of a deaf person in their life.

We identified five specific user groups with an immediate, high-stakes incentive to learn:

### 1. Frontline Service Workers
Bank tellers, metro station staff, government desk officers, and grocery store cashiers encounter deaf customers regularly with no way to communicate. They need a focused set of transactional signs: "Insert PIN," "Show ID," "Sign here," "Go to Counter 4." Not a full course — just 20 hyper-specific signs that make their job possible.

### 2. New Parents of Deaf Children
90% of deaf children are born to hearing parents. When a child is diagnosed, parents urgently need to communicate — but existing ISL resources are textual, dense, and overwhelming. A gamified app that breaks early-stage communication into friendly, low-stress modules removes the barrier to getting started.

### 3. Teachers and Staff in Inclusive Schools
In schools that integrate deaf students, usually only the special educator knows ISL. The principal, supervisor, bus driver, and classroom teacher do not. This isolates deaf children during everyday school activities. Basic classroom phrases — "Sit down," "Open your book," "Line up" — would make an immediate difference.

### 4. Healthcare Professionals
When a deaf patient arrives at an emergency room, communication barriers become dangerous. Writing notes is slow. Medical interpreters are rarely available. Misreading a symptom can cause serious harm. Clinical micro-modules covering critical diagnostic signs — "Where does it hurt?", "Chest pain," "Breathing trouble," "Allergy," "Take this medicine twice a day" — could be lifesaving.

### 5. Corporate Teams and Coworkers
Companies hire deaf employees to comply with diversity frameworks or the Rights of Persons with Disabilities Act — but those employees often get isolated because coworkers don't know how to communicate with them. Brief team modules covering workplace signs ("Good morning," "Review this," "Meeting at 3 PM," "Great job") turn workplace inclusion from a policy into a practice.

## Why this is sustainable

A fair challenge: if a bank teller only needs 20 signs and then leaves the app, how does this work long-term?

Signovate is built on a **Just-In-Time Utility Model** combined with **B2B upstream retention**. Instead of chasing daily active users like a social media app, it tracks Completion Efficiency and Micro-Retention.

Individual users may complete their module and leave. But the institutions managing them do not:

```
[State Bank of India / Metro Corporation]
         ↓ mandates training
[Batch 1 of Tellers] → learn 30 signs in 3 days → achieve competence → exit
         ↓ next month
[Batch 2 of New Hires] → same path → same outcome
```

Meanwhile, parents and school staff become long-term users — continuously expanding a child's vocabulary, and school staff encountering new groups of deaf students every academic year.

## Why existing solutions failed

### Sign Learn — The Government App (by ISLRTC)
A massive, clunky database. User reviews consistently report the native video player crashing, failing to buffer on lower-tier networks, and refusing to play entirely. There is no curriculum, no guided learning path, and no assessment. It is a digital textbook, not a learning tool.

### DEF-ISL — The NGO App (Deaf Enabled Foundation)
Highly respectable with a large vocabulary bank — but its entire experience is designed for deaf individuals to look up language mechanics. A hearing person opening the app is immediately confronted with walls of complex signs and no clear starting point. It assumes prior knowledge of ISL fundamentals.

### ISL Journey — The International Spin-off
Introduces decent gamification, but locks extended learning behind aggressive monthly and yearly paywalls. It lacks regional vocabulary variations and offers no job-specific training modules tailored to Indian infrastructure — no public sector bank counter, no Indian railway station context. A one-size-fits-all model that fits no one particularly well.

## How Signovate Fixes This

| Problem | Our Solution |
|---|---|
| Government app videos don't load | Re-host ISLRTC videos as compressed clips that load reliably on low-bandwidth connections |
| Existing apps target deaf users only | Platform is designed for hearing users starting from zero |
| No structured learning path | Lessons organized into 5 job-specific categories matching real user needs |
| Expensive paywalls | Completely free |
| Complex vocabulary dumps | 2-minute interactive modules with low cognitive load |

---

## How It Works

**Inside a module**, signs play one at a time — a short video loops continuously, sourced directly from ISLRTC's official government dictionary, so the learner can watch as many times as needed before moving on.

**After a small group of signs**, the learner is quizzed immediately on what they just saw. Each question shows the sign again and asks for its meaning from a set of options. Wrong answers are queued for a retry and must be answered correctly twice before being cleared.

**Progress is visible immediately** — how many signs are complete, how many quizzes passed, what percentage of the module remains. A learner always knows exactly where they are and how close they are to finishing.
